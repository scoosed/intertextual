Fallout Intertextual Essay

By Blake Verner

Introduction Shared Setting Form & Audience Conclusion

Introduction

The Fallout franchise, spanning both an iconic video game series and a recent television adaptation, presents a unique and satire vision of a post apocalyptic America. Both texts reside in the same universe: a futuristic world that is culturally frozen in the 1950s atomic age, which decays in a devastating nuclear holocaust. The franchise has many themes, such as uncontrolled consumerism, nationalism, in an apocalyptic setting and the anxieties of the nuclear era remains in both the Fallout game series and the TV show as they are set in the same universe. Both texts explore these ideas & themes, they differ in their form, narrative structure, and audience engagement, ultimately converting the desired emotions and reaction to the audience.

Shared Setting & Themes

The similarity between the Fallout game series and the TV show is their shared setting and themes. Both texts are in the same alternative timeline where cultural and political anxieties of the 1940s and 50s (The golden age of america after WWII) such as anti communist fear, resource scarcity, and a naive faith in nuclear power never disappeared with the innovation of the 1970s and modern technology because they discovered in this timeline how to harness atomic energy as a power source and therefore leading to different branching of cultural and international technological advancements and development throughout time, which ultimately with all this international consumerism and golden age, lead to a brutal war conflict between the major powers of the world as they fought over the limited resources they had depleted with all the consumerism. The video games immerse the player in this setting through development; the player in most of the games starts in a vault, and ventures out of the vault to discover the cruel reality of the wasteland (which the TV show also began with this) actively discovers the remains of pre-war propaganda ("fight the reds"), listens to the culturally remaining 1950s era music on the radio, and encounters the remnants of a society built on American exceptionalism. The TV show shares this exact purpose and context, a complete adaption of this and its underlying themes. It begins with the main protagonist, Lucy, a naive vault dweller, who has lived in the vault her whole life, unknown about just how screwed up the world is on the surface, who ventures out into the wasteland and her optimistic, prewar "Vault Tec" golden age lalalalala happy happy worldview with the harsh reality and cruelness of the wasteland as she explores the decaying ruins of the world before the war. Both texts use this Atomic Age as a sort of satire to the "American Dream," showing its true nature, a fake, hollow promise and the catastrophic consequences of the corporate greed that defined that era.

Form & Audience

The texts are different in their form and the way they structure the audience's experience. The Fallout game series, as a collection of open world role-playing games (a RPG), is defined by player agency and its famous form of build your own adventure, letting the player form their own story individually. Its text form is interactive; the game includes unique dialogue, environmental storytelling found in computer terminals, and a morality system where the player’s choices directly shape the narrative into their own individual story. The target audience is an active participant who, like the vault dweller, is pushed into the wasteland and must "discover" the world and their place in it. On the other hand, the Fallout TV series is a linear, non-interactive narrative where the story is already set. Its form is greatly cinematic using structured plotting, character arcs, (the BIGGEST plot twist ever) and deliberate pacing to tell a specific story. The audience is merely an observer, following the intersecting paths of Lucy, The Ghoul, and Maximus, As the show starts them all in their own separate life and eventually their lives collide. This difference in form fundamentally alters the theme: the games ask the player what they would do in the wasteland, while the show tells the audience a story about what happened in it.

Conclusion

In conclusion, both the games and the show are effective in achieving their purpose, but their different forms lead to different conclusions about how that message is conveyed. The effectiveness of the game series depends on player immersion. By forcing the player to make difficult choices, such as deciding the fate of a settlement or choosing which faction to side with, the games make the player personally feel like they are truly interacting with the themes. The endings are unique and player dependent. The TV show’s effectiveness, storytelling, in contrast, depends on the story, focus & clarity.It can deliver sharper, more focused themes by controlling the narrative and story. For example, the show’s flashbacks to the pre-war era explicitly reveal the insane corporate conspiracy and control behind the apocalypse itself, a piece of lore that is only hinted at or scattered across dozens of hours in the games (which confirms things referenced in the games). This makes the show more effective at conveying the story and themes of the corruption that, ultimately, created the wasteland, making the franchise's complex ideas more accessible for a broader audience that may never have played the games.
